How It Began

Fantasy has been present since humanity first came into existence. Ancient folktales from India and China were told orally, and often told of the the heoric actions of a few special or supernatural individuals, be they people or animals. Epics such as The Epic of Gilgamesh (pictured above), the Ramayana and the Mahabharata are described by some as being some of the earliest stories which incorporated elements of fantasy into their narratives. Greek mythology was also influential in the development of fantasy; the tales of many powers of the gods and goddesses that dwelled on Mount Olympus continue to enthrall scholars, artists and storytellers to this day. The old Norse religion futher popularized the concepts of gods, spirits and many realms of magic where powerful beings existed while all being linked together. The Old English epic, Beowulf, is the best known work of Anglo-Saxon literature, and has influenced the modern English language as well as the development of the concept of heroes.

Emergence of High Fantasy

19th Century classics, such as The Well at the World's End, Alice's Adventures in Wonderland, and The Jungle Book all opened the gates for more fantastical stories. Weird Tales, the first magazine that featured solely fantasy stories, was published in 1923. J.R.R. Tolkien, a professor at the University of Oxford, wrote The Hobbit and published it on 21 September, 1937. This novel, originally meant for children, went on to have sequels in The Lord of the Rings, which were published in the 1950s. Tolkien is widely credited to have created modern high fantasy, having created entire languages from scratch for his work. C.S. Lewis, a close friend of Tolkien, wrote The Chronicles of Narnia, publishing them from the early to the late 1950s. Many other writers followed suit, creating worlds in their brilliant writings, inspiring millions around the world to pick up their pens and write, as well.
